About Me

Overview

I am a Ph.D. candidate in the School of Sustainable Engineering and the Built Environment at Arizona State University (ASU) specializing in Hydrosystems Engineering. My research focuses on different aspects of urban hydrology ranging from climate to infrastructure. It addresses a fundamental challenge in urban resilience: understanding how extreme weather events propagate through interconnected infrastructure systems that were designed to operate independently.

I work at the intersection of climate science, infrastructure modeling, and resilience engineering, developing integrated approaches that capture the full spectrum of urban hydrological systems—from the climate variables during storms that cause damage and trigger cascading failures across power, stormwater, and transportation networks. My work challenges the traditional siloed approach to infrastructure design by creating mechanistic frameworks that represent the complex interdependencies between systems and their responses to spatiotemporally variable climate forcings. By combining high-resolution modeling of climate extremes with computational simulation of infrastructure behavior, I aim to transform how cities anticipate, prepare for, and adapt to the intensifying storms of a changing climate, ultimately contributing to more resilient and sustainable urban environments.

I am affiliated with the Hydroclimate & Infrastructure Research Lab with Prof. Giuseppe Mascaro and the Resilient Infrastructure Laboratory with Prof. Mikhail Chester.

Research

Publications

Optimizing Infrastructure Resilience Strategies for Storm-Vulnerable Systems

Authors: Nehal Ansh Srivastava, Mikhail Chester, Mattheus Porto, and Giuseppe Mascaro

In Preparation.

Key Takeaway: Implementing Fail-Safe and Safe-to-Fail design in mechanistic models of stormwater and power infrastructure to determine optimal placement and improve resilience using vulnerability indicators of service disruption.

Importance of High-Resolution Wind Gust Modeling to Improve Urban Flood Risk Assessment

Authors: Nehal Ansh Srivastava, Mikhail Chester, and Giuseppe Mascaro

In Preparation.

Key Takeaway: Testing and identifying high-resolution wind gust interpolation methodology that leverages LiDAR-derived elevation, building footprints, and land cover data to overcome weather station limitations at infratsructure relevant heights for advancing the capability to model cascading infrastructure failures in cities.

Expanding infrastructure resilience horizons: Safe-to-Fail and Social-Ecological-Technological dynamics for requisite variety

Authors: Mattheus Porto, Mikhail Chester, Yeowon Kim, Giuseppe Mascaro, Nehal Ansh Srivastava

Under Review.

Key Takeaway: Integrates Safe-to-Fail design theory with the Social-Ecological-Technological Systems framework to create a practical failure-management approach that diversifies infrastructure adaptation strategies beyond technology-centered solutions, while identifying necessary governance changes—including cross-departmental collaboration, stakeholder engagement, and new tradeoff navigation methods—to implement this approach across different infrastructure types and geographical contexts.

A coupled stormwater-power model for the simulation of cascading infrastructure failures: An application in Phoenix, AZ

Authors: Nehal Ansh Srivastava, Ryan Sparks, Mikhail Chester, Mattheus Porto, Nathan Johnson, and Giuseppe Mascaro

Under Review.

Key Takeaway: Developed and applied the first integrated SWMM-PyPSA modeling platform to simulate how wind-induced damage to power infrastructure components propagates and causes cascading failures in urban infrastructure.

Changing boundaries, distributed control, and implications for transportation sustainability

Authors: Eleanor M. Hennessy, J. Robert Wolfinbarger, Irfan Batur, Nehal Ansh Srivastava, Mattheus Porto, Gabriel I. Cotlier, Madison Horgan, Ian Searles, Ryan M. Sparks & Mikhail V. Chester

npj. Sustain. Mobil. Transp. 1, 12 (2024). (DOI: 10.1038/s44333-024-00016-2)

Key Takeaway: Explored how decentralized control strategies in complex urban systems, like transportation networks, affect overall system sustainability and operational efficiency under changing conditions.

Improving the utility of weather radar for the spatial frequency analysis of extreme precipitation

Authors: Nehal Ansh Srivastava and Giuseppe Mascaro

Journal of Hydrology, Volume 624, 2023. (DOI: 10.1016/j.jhydrol.2023.129902)

Key Takeaway: Developed a robust statistical framework to use high-resolution radar data (QPE) for improving the accuracy of Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) curves, which are essential for resilient urban stormwater planning.

Additional Research & Projects

Uncertainty Analysis of Performance of Stormwater Infrastructure

Analyzed stormwater infrastructure behavior using an SWMM model in response to projected deep uncertainties, conducting robustness and flexibility analysis via a scenario development approach.

Analysis of Floodplain Accuracy using Remotely Sensed Data

Utilized areal imagery from Planet for classification of flooded area using supervised and unsupervised classification algorithms, comparing performance against the FEMA floodplain dataset.

Analysis of Impact of Urbanization on Runoff Generation

Analyzed changes in runoff quantity in a residential area developed over 20 years by developing a KINEROS hydrologic model.

MATLAB Software Development to Analyze Aquifer Characteristics

Developed a generalized MATLAB-based software to derive aquifer characteristics through numerical solutions of theoretical equations using data from pumping tests.
